Definition 2.2 - Ring Homomorphism

Let R and S be rings. φ : R → S is said to be a ring homomorphism  ⇔ ∀a, b ∈ R

  • φ(a + b) = φ(a) + φ(b)

  • φ(a ⋅ b) = φ(a) ⋅ φ(b)

Lemma 2.4 - Composition of Ring Homomorphisms

The composition of two ring homomorphisms is a ring homomorphism.

Lemma 2.5 - Ring Homomorphism Rules

Suppoe that φ : R → S is a ring homomorphism.

  • ∀a, b ∈ R, φ(b − a) = φ(b) − φ(a)

  • φ(0R) = 0Swhere 0 is the additive identity. Note that this is not necessarily true with 1 (the multiplicative identity).

  • ∀a ∈ R, φ( − a) =  − φ(a)

Proposition 2.7 - Evaluation Maps are Ring Homomorphisms

Suppose that R is a commutative ring and r ∈ R. evr : R[t] → R is a ring homomorphism.

Definition 2.13 - Quotient Map

Let I be an ideal in a ring R. The quotient map π : R → R/I maps a ring to a coset on I, where π(a) = a + I for a ∈ R.

Definition 2.14 - Inclusion Map

Let R be a ring and S be a subring where 1 ∈ S

inc : S → R where inc(s) = s ∈ R; the same element but considered as an element of R since S ⊆ R.

Lemma 2.15 - REDO

Let R be a ring. Let I be an ideal in R. Let S be a subring of R with 1 ∈ S.

  • π : R → R/I is a surjective ring homomorphism, so  = R/I and  = I

  • ι : S → R is an injective ring homomorphism, so  = {0} and  = S

Theorem 2.16 - Emulating a homomorphism with Quotients

Suppose that φ : R → S is a ring homomorphism and let I be an ideal in R satisfying I⊆. Then there exists a unique ring homomorphism $\overline{\varphi}:R/I\to S$ where $\overline{\varphi}\circ\pi=\varphi$ and π : R → R/I.

We define $\overline{\varphi}:R/I\to S$ by setting $\overline{\varphi}(a+I)=\varphi(a)$ - this allows the map to be well defined, and to be a homomorphism by force.

Definition 2.17- Ring Isomorphisms

Consider the homomorphism φ : R → S. φ is isomorphic  ⇔ ∃ψ : S → R where ψ(φ(r)) = r and φ(ψ(s)) = s. Namely, ψ is a left and right inverse for φ.

Now, φ does not necessarily have to be a ring homomorphism.

Definition 2.29 - Ideal operations

Let R be a ring and let I, J be ideals of R

  • Sum: I + J := {a+b:a∈I,b∈J} ⊆ R (since ideals are subrings)

  • Product: $IJ=\left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{k}a_{i}b_{i}:k\in\mathbb{N},a_{i}\in I,b_{i}\in J\right\} \subseteq R$(sums of all possible sequences in I and J)

  • Intersection: I ∩ J = {a : a ∈ I, a ∈ J} ⊆ R

Lemma 2.30 - Properties of Ideal Operations

  • Suppose that I and J are ideals.

    • The sum (I + J), the product (IJ) and the intersection (I ∩ J) are all ideals of R.

    • IJ ⊆ I ∩ J ⊆ I + J

Definition 2.33 - Direct Product

Suppose R and S are rings.

R × S = {(r,s):r∈R,s∈S}

is a RING called the Direct Product of R and S where operations are defined as follows:

  • Addition: (a, b) + (c, d) = (a + c, b + d)

  • Mulitplication: (a, b) ⋅ (c, d) = (ac, bd)

Theorem 2.34 - Chinese Remainder Theorem

Let I, J be ideals in a ring satisfying I + J = R. Then there is a ring isomorphism R/(I ∩ J) ≅ (R/I) × (R/J)
